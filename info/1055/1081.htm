Neuromorphic computing

APPT

(Chaired by Songwen Pei (swpei@usst.edu.cn), Elena Goi, University of Shanghai for Science and Technology, China)

In the big data era, artificial intelligence (AI) and in particular machine learning (ML) have an enormous impact on every sector of our lives. The latest breakthroughs in ML have been fueled by the processing power and parallelism of modern graphics processing units (GPUs). However, high-end accelerators running increasingly complex neural networks are hungry for power and bandwidth, require substantial processing times and bulky form factors. Analog ML hardware platforms promise to be faster and more energy efficient than their digital counterparts and wave physics, as found in acoustics and optics, is a natural candidate for building analog processors.

This targeted workshop will bring together researchers from computer science, electronic engineering, optics and photonics to consider analog computing platforms, which use the natural evolution of a continuous physical system to perform calculations, and more specifically to implement neuromorphic architectures that mimic the biological circuitry of the brain.

The major topics include:

Ø analog neuromorphic computing

Ø memristive computing

Ø optical neural networks

Ø optical spiking networks

Ø diffractive neural networks

Ø Etc.

报告题目：Integrated high-neuron-density optical perceptrons performing near-infrared inference

报告摘要：Optical machine learning has emerged as an important research area that, by leveraging the advantages inherent to optical signals such as parallelism and high speed, paves the way for a future where optical hardware can process data at the speed of light. In this work, such optical devices for data processing in the form of single-layer and multi-layer nanoscale holographic perceptrons trained to perform optical inference tasks are presented. We show the functionality of these passive optical devices on the example of decryptors trained to perform optical inference of single or whole classes of keys through symmetric and asymmetric decryption and multi-layer perceptrons for wavefront aberration detection. The perceptrons, designed for operation in the near-infrared region, are nanoprinted on complementary metal-oxide–semiconductor chips by galvo-dithered two-photon nanolithography with axial nanostepping of 10 nm, achieving a neuron density of more than 500 million neurons per square centimetre. The compact form factor of the resulting perceptrons and the lithographic fabrication technology that allow for directly integration on opto-electronic sensors, enable the co-integration of the optical perceptrons with additional layers of electronic neural networks, or the use of the sensor’s nonlinear response as nonlinear activation function, in this way forming deep neural networks. This power-efficient commixture of machine learning and on-chip integration may have a transformative impact on optical decryption, sensing, microscopy, high-precision laser nanolithography, medical diagnostics, and computing.
